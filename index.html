Cases — Sanctions & Hallucinations

  • Cassata v. Michael Macrina Architect, P.C., N.Y. Sup. Ct. (2026) [PDF] Denial of AI use; 12-page appendix cataloging AI sanctions cases. Circumstantial detection of AI-generated content.
  • Noland v. Land of the Free, L.P., 114 Cal. App. 5th 426 (Sept. 12, 2025) [PDF] Attorney used ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok; 21 of 23 quotations fabricated. $10,000 sanction and State Bar referral. Documents multi-platform hallucination.
  • Nuvola, Minn. Dist. Ct., Hennepin Cnty. (Nov. 20, 2025) [PDF] GenAI-fabricated citations. Sanctions include OLPR referral, 5 required CLE presentations on AI risks, and $1,000 fine. Court cited 509 documented cases of fake AI citations.
  • Whiting v. City of Athens, Nos. 24-5918/5919/25-5424, 6th Cir. (Mar. 13, 2026) [PDF] Published opinion. Most severe federal appellate sanctions for AI hallucinations: 24+ fake citations, $15,000 per attorney, double costs, fees, disciplinary referral. Attorneys accused court of conspiracy.
  • Doiban v. Oregon Liquor & Cannabis Comm'n, 347 Or. App. 742 (Mar. 18, 2026) [PDF] Per-unit sanctions formula: $500 per fabricated citation, $1,000 per fabricated quotation. 15 fabricated citations + 9 fabricated quotations. $10,000 (capped from $16,500 baseline).
  • Creditors Adjustment Bureau v. All Season Power LLC, No. 2:25-cv-09888, C.D. Cal. (Feb. 13, 2026) [PDF] Both sides cited fake cases. Plaintiff admitted AI use; defendant "followed plaintiff's counsel over the cliff." Court's "Thelma and Louise" metaphor. Warning but no sanctions.
  • Sanchez v. 450 Grand Ave. Owner LLC, 2026 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 1620, N.Y. Sup. Ct., Kings Cnty. (Mar. 2026) [PDF] New vector: fabricated deposition testimony (not citations). Purported testimony "does not exist in the record." Serial offender; sanctions hearing scheduled.
  • Amtrust N. Am. v. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co., 2026 N.J. Super. Unpub. LEXIS 633, N.J. Super. App. Div. (Mar. 27, 2026) [PDF] $1,000 personal sanction. 4 nonexistent cases; counsel doubled down after opposition flagged errors. "Attorneys have a duty of candor to the tribunal that cannot be outsourced to AI."
  • Labatt USA Operating Co. v. Friends Bev. Grp., 2025 N.Y. Misc. LEXIS 10738, N.Y. Sup. Ct. (Dec. 30, 2025) [PDF] Opposition to summary judgment stricken for AI misuse, leaving motion unopposed.

Cases — Cover-Up & Doubling Down

  • Flycatcher Corp. v. Affable Avenue LLC, S.D.N.Y. (Feb. 5, 2026, Judge Failla) [PDF] First default judgment for AI misuse. Attorney "tripled down" with opening and reply briefs containing additional fake citations after initial Order to Show Cause.
  • Kjoller v. Superior Court of Nevada County, Cal. Supreme Court (2026) [PDF — Jenner & Block analysis] DA submitted brief with 8 fabricated/misrepresented cases, characterized them as "scrivener's errors." Cal. Supreme Court directed OSC and referee investigation. Pattern: denial transforms correctable mistakes into career-ending investigations.
  • In re Domestic Partnership of Torres Campos & Munoz, No. D085584, Cal. App. 4th Dist. (Mar. 5, 2026) [PDF] Fictitious cases sourced from Reddit by non-lawyer blogger. Fake citations infected trial court's own order. $5,000 sanction + State Bar referral. Published to emphasize courts' duty to protect against fabricated law.
  • Cartagena v. Dixon, S.D.N.Y. (Mar. 10, 2026) [PDF] Serial offender across 4+ courts. Blamed LexisNexis "Protégé" tool, but LexisNexis submitted letter refuting he had a subscription. Continued filing fake cites after completing AI CLE courses.
  • State v. Coleman, 2026-Ohio-965, Ohio 11th App. Dist. (Mar. 20, 2026) [PDF] Murder case: paralegal's ChatGPT fabricated transcript quotations. Attorney's remedial "AI policy" was itself AI-generated with "[Insert Date]" placeholders. Subsequent filing contained embedded ChatGPT prompt. Appealed without disclosing fabrication.
  • McCarthy v. DEA, 3d Cir. (Mar. 27, 2026) [PDF] First circuit-level competence violation for AI. Attorney incorporated AI-generated case summaries from non-attorney without checking. Doubled down after adversary flagged errors. Reprimand + National Disciplinary Data Bank notification.

Cases — Federal Appellate Discipline

  • In re Eric Chibueze Nwaubani, No. 25-9517, 4th Cir. (Mar. 11, 2026) [PDF] First federal appellate disciplinary opinion for hallucinated citations. Technology-agnostic: "Rule 8.4(d)'s prohibition applies to submitting a brief with nonexistent cases no matter how it is done, whether through generative AI or not."
  • Fivehouse v. Secretary of Defense, E.D.N.C. (Mar. 20, 2026) [PDF] AUSA used AI after losing draft; fabricated regulatory quotes and case citations. Errors caught by pro se plaintiff (retired Air Force colonel), not DOJ. AUSA resigned; DOJ fired next day.

Cases — Privilege & Discovery

  • United States v. Heppner, No. 1:25-cr-00503-JSR, S.D.N.Y. (Feb. 17, 2026, Judge Rakoff) [PDF] Question of first impression: criminal defendant's communications with Claude not protected by attorney-client privilege or work product. Claude is not an attorney; no reasonable expectation of confidentiality.
  • Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc., No. 2:24-cv-12333, E.D. Mich. (Feb. 10, 2026, Mag. Judge Patti) [PDF] Denied demand for documents on plaintiff's AI use. Work product protection applies. "Defendants' preoccupation with Plaintiff's use of AI needs to abate."
  • Scott A. Milner & Matthew J. Hamilton, "When AI Meets Privilege: Early Court Decisions," Morgan Lewis LawFlash (Feb. 18, 2026) [PDF] Practitioner synthesis of Heppner and Warner. Key insight: consumer AI may waive privilege; enterprise AI directed by counsel may yield different result per Rakoff's dictum.
  • Courtney L. Baird & Ryan S. Crawford, "The Perils of Privilege Waivers Through AI," Duane Morris (Mar. 4, 2026) [PDF] Broader waiver risk: not just AI outputs lose privilege — underlying privileged materials inputted into consumer AI may lose protection too.

Cases — Pro Se Litigants & AI

  • Hilpert v. 16 Judge SPV LLC, N.Y. Sup. Ct., Kings Cnty. (Dec. 4, 2025) [PDF] Pro se: 12 fake citations + 7 fake quotes. Attorneys' fees + vexatious litigant declaration + filing restrictions. Court cited Dowlah (1st Dept.): pro se status no excuse.
  • Biglow v. Dell Technologies, 10th Cir. (2026) [PDF] Pro se AI warning, no sanctions on first instance. Liberal construction applied. Fabricated citations in multiple filings.
  • Jones v. Kankakee County Sheriff's Dep't, No. 25-1251, 7th Cir. (Jan. 21, 2026) [PDF] Court noted 60% of 7th Circuit caseload includes at least one pro se party. Declined to sanction on first instance. Professional formatting cited as evidence of AI use.
  • Kabir v. WebMD LLC, D.N.J. (2026) [PDF] E-filing privileges revoked; pre-filing injunction threatened. Hallucinated citations. Procedural throttle for pro se AI abuse.
  • Leber v. Bryan Medical Center, D. Neb. (Dec. 23, 2025) [PDF] Pro se: repeated citation to hallucinated case despite prior warning and certification of verification. Future briefs may be stricken + sanctions.
  • USA v. Payseur, D.N.J. (Jan. 7, 2026) [PDF] AI use not inherently sanctionable. Verification duty applies to all litigants. Important modulation of per se approaches.
  • Augustin v. Formula 3 Brooklyn Inc., N.Y. Sup. Ct., Kings Cnty. (2025) [PDF] AI certification enforcement. Pro se admitted ChatGPT use. UPL issue (nonlawyer representing parent).
  • "Pro Se Pal" — N.D.N.Y. Bar Association AI Chatbot Program [PDF] Court-approved bounded AI chatbot for pro se litigants. NDNY bar association initiative designed to limit hallucination risk while expanding access to justice.

Cases — Other AI Issues

  • Fortis Advisors LLC v. Krafton, Inc., C.A. No. 2025-0805-LWW, Del. Ch. (Mar. 16, 2026) [PDF] $500M+ acquisition dispute. CEO "consulted an artificial intelligence chatbot to contrive a corporate 'takeover' strategy." AI used for corporate decision-making, not hallucination.
  • Matter of M.S. (M.H.), 2026 N.Y. Slip Op. 00825, N.Y. Ct. App. (2026) [PDF] Deepfake evidence authentication. Few qualified forensic experts; prohibitive costs for local agencies. Signals emerging challenge for video/media evidence.
  • Rako v. VMware LLC, 2026 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 23692, N.D. Cal. (2026) [PDF] Mutual AI accusations as litigation tactic. Court established meet-and-confer requirement for AI disputes. Procedural framework for weaponized AI allegations.
  • Coomer v. Lindell, No. 22-cv-01129-NYW-SBP, D. Colo. [PDF] Defamation case with AI-related elements.
  • Prososki v. Regan, 321 Neb. 38 (Mar. 20, 2026) [PDF] Nebraska Supreme Court. Family law appeal. AI relevance under review.
  • Andrew Fleischman, X thread on Payne v. State, Ga. Supreme Court oral argument (Mar. 18, 2026) [PDF] Prosecutor submitted proposed order with AI hallucinations; trial court signed it. Prosecutor blamed trial court for adding the fake cites, but same hallucinations appeared in her own brief. 113K views.
  • "Federal judge withdraws opinion after lawyer points out fake quotes, misstated case outcomes" (2026) [PDF] D.N.J. judge withdrew opinion after counsel identified fake quotes and misstated outcomes. Confirms CorMedix pattern of judicial AI errors.

Articles & Practitioner Analysis

  • "The Claude-Native Law Firm: How I Actually Practice Law with AI in 2026" [PDF] Boutique transactional lawyer built two-person firm around Claude. Custom skills, real-time contract interpretation, privilege analysis via agent/instrumentality exception.
  • Michael Showalter, "How I Use Claude To Draft Legal Scholarship At 10x," X thread (Mar. 23, 2026) [PDF] Anonymous author of forthcoming SMU Law Review Forum article revealed. ~15 hours vs. prior ~150 hours. Coins "ad machinem fallacy." 250K+ views on original post.
  • Adam Tauman Kalai et al., "Why Language Models Hallucinate," arXiv (Sept. 4, 2025) (OpenAI / Georgia Tech) [PDF] Mathematical framework: hallucinations are structurally inevitable. Pretraining on error-containing corpora + evaluation benchmarks that reward guessing over abstention = systematic hallucination pressure.
  • Ankit Maloo, "Experts Have World Models. LLMs Have Word Models," Latent.Space (Feb. 7, 2026) [PDF] LLMs operate on statistical word patterns, not true understanding. Fails in adversarial multi-agent environments like litigation. Relevant counterpoint to optimistic AI adoption.
  • Jaime Teevan, "AI Isn't Human," AI by Hand / Substack (Mar. 7, 2026) (Chief Scientist, Microsoft) [PDF] Five ways AI differs from humans. Key insight: "AI can operate boldly and at scale, but it can't be held responsible. It can't lose its job, face consequences, or feel the weight of a bad decision."
  • Amanda Robert, "May It Please the Court: How do judges use generative AI?," ABA Journal (Jan. 2026) [PDF] Federal judges in Mississippi and New Jersey withdrew rulings with AI errors. Judge Schlegel: "When lawyers make mistakes, they get sanctioned. When judges do it, it becomes the law."
  • Elisa Reiter & Daniel Pollack, "Navigating the AI-Informed Client," N.Y. Law Journal (Mar. 23, 2026) [PDF] The "AI-informed client" phenomenon: clients arrive with confident but jurisdiction-blind AI answers, resist attorney counsel that conflicts with the chatbot's "tidy answer."
  • Margarita Loktionova, "We Analyzed 89K LinkedIn URLs Cited in AI Search," Semrush Blog (Mar. 10, 2026) [PDF] Reddit is #1 domain cited by AI search (11.29%). Legal databases absent from top 20. Explains the Torres Campos propagation vector.
  • Laban et al., "Why Language Models Get 'Lost' in Conversation," Microsoft Research / Salesforce (May 2025) [PDF] LLM performance degrades 39% in multi-turn conversations. Top models degrade equally to weak ones. Models lock onto incorrect assumptions and compound errors across turns.
  • Ava Mandoli, "Bridging the Gap: Path to Practice Survey," Bloomberg Law (Winter 2026) [PDF] 76% of attorneys expect new hires to verify AI citations on Day One. Only 40% of students gained AI proficiency in school. 56% of attorneys: "AI is growing faster than I can keep up."
  • James Queally, "AI pilot program in L.A. County courts will help judges craft rulings," L.A. Times (Mar. 18, 2026) [PDF] 6 judges, 10 states, Learned Hand "judicial sous chef." $300K contract. Anonymous judge: AI tentative "psychologically becomes your reference point." No disclosure requirement.
  • "Designed to Cross: Why Nippon Life v. OpenAI Is a Product Liability Case," Stanford Law School CodeX [PDF] Frames AI UPL as product liability rather than professional conduct. CDA § 230 implications. Proposes certification regime for legal AI tools.
  • "Pro Se Advocacy in the AI Era: Benefits, Challenges, and Ethical Implications," NYSBA (2026) [PDF] 83,000 NY pro se litigants. AI avatar in oral argument. Pro Se Pal. Proposed pro se AI affirmation. Two-tiered justice concern.
  • "AI Use by Self-Represented Litigants — Recent Developments (Oct 2025 – March 2026)," Deep Research Report [PDF] Comprehensive research: fractured discovery rulings, weaponization of AI for vexatious litigation, legislative efforts to impose tort liability on AI platforms for UPL.
  • "When courts meet GenAI: Guiding self-represented litigants through the AI maze," Thomson Reuters Institute [PDF] NCSC/TRI consortium approach. Court guidance, purpose-built legal AI tools, self-help center AI training, staff equipping vs. litigant teaching.

Policy, Rules & Legislation

  • ABA Task Force on Law and Artificial Intelligence — Year 2 Report (Dec. 2025) [PDF] Comprehensive 50-page analysis: 10 major areas including Sedona Conference guidelines (reprinted), law practice, courts, access to justice, legal education, governance, risk management, bar ethics, dispute resolution, model contract terms.
  • ABA AI Legislation Tracker (current as of Feb. 6, 2026) [PDF] State-by-state and federal tracker. 40+ states and Congress across 12 categories. Highlights EO 231, California SB 574 (attorney verification), and New York's comprehensive AI bills.
  • "President Donald J. Trump Unveils National AI Legislative Framework," White House (Mar. 20, 2026) [PDF] Seven-pillar federal framework. Explicitly seeks federal preemption of state AI laws: "a patchwork of conflicting state laws would undermine American innovation." Escalation from EO 231.
  • NY Advisory Committee on AI and the Courts — Annual Report (Dec. 2025) [PDF] Most comprehensive policy document in collection. Interim policy, proposed Part 161 model rule (opposes blanket bans, requires meaningful disclosure, recommends against certification), six subcommittee reports.
  • NY Unified Court System Interim Policy on AI (eff. Oct. 2025) [PDF] 8 requirements/restrictions for court personnel. Approved tools: Azure, CoPilot Chat, GitHub CoPilot, Trados, ChatGPT (free version only).
  • In re Generative Artificial Intelligence, 2026 OK 19, Oklahoma Supreme Court (Mar. 23, 2026) [PDF] Permissive statewide order. AI use "is expected" and "should not be discouraged." Disclosure "shall not be required." Follows Illinois model.
  • California Judicial Council AI Task Force — Model Policy Presentation (Feb. 21, 2025, Hon. Mary J. Greenwood) [PDF] Court survey: 42% already using GenAI. Model policy template covering confidentiality, supervision, bias, transparency. Backstory for California Standard 10.80.
  • "Council Receives Preview of New Model Policy," California Courts Newsroom (Feb. 21, 2025) [PDF] Chief Justice Guerrero: "This is a positive step in a rapidly developing area." Transparency requirement for AI outputs that make up "a substantial portion" of written work.

CLE Materials & Judicial Guidelines

  • "Writing for the Courts in the Age of AI," ABA BLS Spring 2025 Panel Slides [PDF] Panelists: Judge Damian (S.D. Fla.), Chancellor Martin (Del. Chancery), Prof. Verret. AI vs. GenAI definitions, permissible uses, risks, Model Rules (1.1, 1.4, 1.6, 3.3, 5.1, 5.3).
  • Hon. Herbert B. Dixon Jr. et al., "Navigating AI in the Judiciary," Sedona Conference Journal, Vol. 26 (2025) [PDF] Guidelines for U.S. judicial officers on responsible AI use. Fundamental principles, capabilities/limitations, potential judicial applications, implementation considerations.
  • Conference of Federal Judges — AI Materials (Jan. 2026) [PDF] Federal judiciary's approach to AI integration, policy considerations, and practical guidance.
  • Hon. Zainab A. Chaudhry & Hon. Denise A. Hartman, "It's Here! AI in Your Chambers & Your Courtroom," NY Court of Claims Annual Meeting (Oct. 28, 2025) [PDF] CLE packet with slides + 110-page appendix. DOs/DON'Ts for judges, case studies, evidence issues, deepfakes, Frye/Daubert for AI evidence, disciplinary considerations.
  • "AI in Judicial Work & Litigation: Key Concepts, Practical Use Patterns, & Courtroom Implications," Fordham CLE Handout [PDF] Concise primer: competency requirements, prompt discipline, model drift, hallucinations, sycophancy, open vs. closed models, judicial guardrails.
